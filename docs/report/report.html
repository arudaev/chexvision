Abstract

Chest radiography is among the most common diagnostic imaging examinations worldwide, yet expert interpretation remains slow, costly, and unevenly available. The public release of the NIH ChestX-ray14 database, comprising 112,120 frontal-view radiographs from 30,805 patients, each weakly labelled with up to fourteen thoracic pathologies mined from the associated radiology reports, has made it possible to study automated pathology detection at hospital scale. In this work we implement and compare two convolutional neural networks on this database under a single, fixed evaluation protocol. The first is a residual CNN with Squeeze-and-Excitation channel attention, trained entirely from scratch. The second is a DenseNet-121 adapted from ImageNet by transfer learning, following the CheXNet design. Both networks share one backbone and two output heads, so that a single forward pass yields a fourteen-class multi-label prediction together with a binary Normal/Abnormal screening decision. On the held-out validation split, the transfer-learned DenseNet attains a macro-averaged AUC-ROC of 0.846, marginally above the published CheXNet benchmark of 0.841, while the from-scratch network reaches 0.801 with no pretrained weights. We report per-class results, examine where the two models agree and where they differ, and discuss the data, modelling, and infrastructure limitations that bound these results. All training is performed on free cloud GPUs from a reproducible, tested, and continuously integrated code base. Although the results are encouraging, fully automated reading of chest radiographs from weakly supervised, report-mined labels remains a difficult task.

1. Introduction

The progress of deep convolutional networks on large annotated image collections has driven substantial gains in object recognition, detection, and segmentation. The medical imaging domain has begun to benefit from the same paradigm, but it poses additional difficulties: pathological findings are often small and of low contrast relative to the full image, expert annotation is expensive, and ground-truth labels are frequently mined from free-text reports rather than verified by region-level markup. Chest radiography is a natural testbed for these challenges. It is the most common imaging exam in clinical practice, and a system that can flag likely pathologies and separate normal from abnormal films would help radiologists prioritise their reading lists and extend basic screening to settings without specialist coverage. This motivation underlies the NIH ChestX-ray14 release (Wang et al., 2017) and the subsequent CheXNet result (Rajpurkar et al., 2017), which reported radiologist-level pneumonia detection on this database and established DenseNet-121 as its reference architecture.

The course brief requires two models on a shared dataset, one implemented and trained from scratch and a second built with an alternative paradigm (transfer learning), together with an explicit quantitative comparison between them. We formulate the problem as two complementary supervised tasks defined on the same images. In the multi-label classification task, the network predicts an independent probability for each of the fourteen pathologies; the labels are not mutually exclusive, so a single film may simultaneously show Effusion, Infiltration, and Atelectasis. This is the harder and clinically richer task, and our primary evaluation target. In the binary classification task, the network predicts a single Normal-versus-Abnormal score indicating whether any finding is present; this is the screening task and a useful auxiliary signal on the shared features.

Both tasks are solved by one network with two output heads on a shared backbone, so that a single forward pass produces both predictions. This design keeps the comparison between the two architectures controlled: only the backbone changes, while the task formulation, loss, data, and evaluation are held fixed. Throughout, we adopt the area under the ROC curve, macro-averaged over the fourteen pathologies, as the primary metric. AUC-ROC is threshold-independent and robust to the severe class imbalance of this dataset, and it is the measure reported by prior work on ChestX-ray14. Accuracy would be uninformative here: a classifier that always predicts the absence of the rarest pathology, Hernia, whose prevalence is well below 1%, attains over 99% accuracy on that class while detecting nothing of clinical value.

2. Dataset and Big-Data Engineering

We use NIH ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al., 2017), one of the largest publicly available labelled chest X-ray collections: 112,120 frontal-view images from 30,805 unique patients, each tagged with zero or more of fourteen pathology labels mined from the radiology reports via natural language processing. The fourteen labels are Atelectasis, Cardiomegaly, Effusion, Infiltration, Mass, Nodule, Pneumonia, Pneumothorax, Consolidation, Edema, Emphysema, Fibrosis, Pleural Thickening, and Hernia. Images with none of these findings are labelled "No Finding".

2.1 Data partition

We randomly shuffle the database once into a fixed 70/10/20 partition for training, validation, and testing, and store the assignment alongside the images so that it is identical across every run and every model. The partition is frozen for reproducibility, and the test split is withheld from all model selection. Table 1 summarises the resulting splits.

2.2 Class imbalance

The dataset is severely imbalanced in two respects. First, roughly 54% of all images carry no finding, so the binary task is close to balanced (about 46% abnormal) while the multi-label task is dominated by negatives. Second, prevalence across the fourteen pathologies spans more than two orders of magnitude: Infiltration and Effusion are common, whereas Hernia appears in well under 1% of films. Left unaddressed, a network minimises its loss most easily by predicting "absent" for every rare class, which is consistent with the difficulty of detecting under-represented findings reported in prior work.

We counteract this directly with per-class positive weighting in the loss (Section 3.2). For each pathology we compute pos_weight = negatives / positives on the training split and pass it to a numerically stable BCEWithLogitsLoss. For the rarest class the positive weight reaches roughly 500, so that a single missed Hernia contributes about as much to the loss as 500 missed Effusions. This is the single most important design choice for obtaining non-trivial performance on the rare tail.

2.3 A pre-resized 320 x 320 dataset

The raw NIH release is approximately 45 GB of 1024 x 1024 PNGs, which is too large to stream efficiently from free cloud GPUs within a practical time budget. We therefore built a one-time preprocessing pipeline, run as a Kaggle kernel, that decodes the raw archives, resizes every image to 320 x 320 RGB, and writes 36 Parquet shards totalling about 7.97 GB, published as a versioned Hugging Face dataset (arudaev/chest-xray-14-320). The dataset revision hash is pinned in source, so that every training run reads byte-identical data. We retain 320 x 320 rather than the common 224-pixel baseline because chest pathology often hinges on small, low-contrast findings such as nodules and fine infiltrates; the higher resolution preserves that detail, at the cost of a smaller batch size to fit GPU memory.

3. Methodology

3.1 Dual-head, single-backbone design

Both models share an identical output design. A shared backbone extracts a feature vector once, and two small linear heads read from it: a multi-label head producing fourteen logits with an independent sigmoid per pathology, and a binary head producing a single Normal-versus-Abnormal logit. This choice is deliberate. Computing both tasks from shared features is cheaper than training two separate networks; the binary task acts as an auxiliary signal that regularises the shared representation; and, most importantly for the comparison, it isolates the variable under study. When we compare the from-scratch model against the DenseNet, only the backbone differs, while the heads, loss, data, and evaluation are held constant.

3.2 Training procedure

Every component below is implemented in raw PyTorch, without a high-level training framework, so that each decision is explicit and defensible. The settings are shared by both models except where Section 5 notes the two-phase schedule specific to the DenseNet.

Table 2. Shared training configuration and the rationale for each choice.
ComponentChoice and justification
LossCombined BCEWithLogitsLoss, 1.0 x multi-label + 0.5 x binary. The fused sigmoid and BCE provide numerical stability; the binary task is down-weighted because it is easier and should support, rather than dominate, the harder multi-label objective.
Class balancingPer-class pos_weight on the multi-label loss, computed from training-split prevalence, to counteract the long-tailed label distribution (Section 2.2). The binary head uses plain BCE because that task is already near-balanced.
OptimizerAdamW with decoupled weight decay of 1e-4, combining Adam-family convergence with proper regularisation; a standard choice for modern vision fine-tuning.
Learning-rate scheduleCosine annealing over the full epoch budget with a short warm-up, smoothly decaying the learning rate towards zero for a stable final solution.
Mixed precisionfp16 forward and backward passes with fp32 master weights. This is about 1.7x faster on the T4/P100 GPUs and roughly halves memory, which is what makes 320-pixel training feasible within the free nine-hour Kaggle window.
Gradient accumulationA physical batch of 24 accumulated over four steps gives an effective batch of 96, recovering the stability of a large batch despite the memory cost of 320-pixel images.
Gradient clippingGlobal-norm clipping at 1.0, to prevent the weighted, imbalanced loss from producing occasional exploding updates on rare-class batches.
ReproducibilityA single seed (42) is set across Python, NumPy, PyTorch, and CUDA, with deterministic cuDNN. Together with the pinned dataset revision, the frozen split, and the embedded configuration, this yields repeatable runs.

Augmentation is chosen to reflect plausible radiographic variation rather than generic image noise, and each transform is justified by a corresponding source of variation in real acquisition (Table 3).

The code base additionally implements two optional enhancements that are driven by configuration. Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalisation (CLAHE) performs local contrast enhancement in LAB colour space to expose low-contrast findings, and label smoothing (epsilon = 0.1) regularises against the dataset's noisy, report-mined labels. As reported transparently in Section 4.4, the final DenseNet checkpoint was trained with both enabled, whereas the from-scratch checkpoint that produced the reported numbers predates their introduction. We state this difference explicitly rather than conceal it, and closing the gap is the first item of future work (Section 6).

4. Models

4.1 Model 1 - custom residual CNN trained from scratch

The first model is a residual convolutional network built and trained entirely from scratch, with no pretrained weights of any kind. Its architecture follows ResNet-50-equivalent depth and adds Squeeze-and-Excitation channel attention; it contains roughly 23 million parameters, all of which are learned on chest X-rays alone.

The stem applies a 7 x 7 convolution at stride 2 followed by batch normalization, ReLU, and 3 x 3 max-pooling, capturing low-level edges and textures while reducing spatial resolution fourfold. Four residual stages of depth [3, 4, 6, 3] follow. We chose ResNet-50 depth over a lighter [2, 2, 2, 2] (ResNet-18) baseline because detecting fourteen simultaneous, often co-occurring pathology signals requires more representational capacity, and the additional depth (about 23M against 11M parameters) is capacity we judged worth its training cost. Each residual block consists of two 3 x 3 convolutions with a skip connection; the residual paths give gradients a short route and are what make a network this deep trainable at all, while batch normalization on every convolution stabilises training and permits a higher learning rate. After each block, a Squeeze-and-Excitation module global-average-pools the feature map to a per-channel descriptor, learns per-channel importance weights through a small two-layer bottleneck with a sigmoid gate, and rescales the channels, performing channel-wise feature selection. A global average pool with dropout (0.5) collapses the spatial dimensions without learned parameters, which is far less prone to overfitting than a large flatten and dense layer, and the dual heads (Linear 512 to 14, and Linear 512 to 1) read from the pooled vector. Convolutions are initialised with Kaiming (He) scaling for the ReLU stack and the linear heads with Xavier; correct variance scaling is essential when training a deep network from random weights.

SE attention is particularly suited to this problem. Different pathologies activate different feature channels, since the texture that signals Emphysema is not the texture that signals Cardiomegaly. The SE modules let the network amplify disease-relevant channels and suppress background-texture channels on a per-image basis, which is the kind of channel competition that multi-label medical imaging creates. The mechanism adds negligible parameters and amounts to learned, disease-specific feature gating (Hu et al., 2018). Training 23 million parameters from random initialisation on a noisy, imbalanced medical dataset is genuinely demanding, because the network must learn edges, textures, and anatomy before it can learn disease and receives no head start from natural-image pretraining. In return, the model provides a clean baseline against which the value of ImageNet transfer can be measured precisely (Section 5).

4.2 Model 2 - DenseNet-121 transfer learning

The second model is a DenseNet-121 pretrained on ImageNet and fine-tuned for our two tasks. It is the alternative paradigm required by the brief and a direct reference to CheXNet (Rajpurkar et al., 2017), which established this backbone as the standard architecture for ChestX-ray14. About 7.9 million parameters sit in the trainable feature and head path on top of the dense backbone.

Three properties motivate this choice. Dense connectivity, in which every layer receives the feature maps of all preceding layers in its block, maximises feature reuse, strengthens gradient flow, and reaches strong accuracy with far fewer parameters than a comparable ResNet, which suits a free-GPU budget. ImageNet pretraining transfers well, because low-level features such as edges, gradients, and blob and texture detectors are largely domain-agnostic; starting from them lets the network spend its budget adapting to radiographic structure rather than relearning vision from scratch. Finally, the backbone is proven on this exact data: CheXNet reported a macro AUC-ROC of 0.841 with a single-head DenseNet-121, giving a credible, literature-anchored target.

4.3 Two-phase fine-tuning

Fine-tuning a pretrained backbone with freshly initialised heads is delicate, because the large early gradients from the random heads can wash out, or catastrophically forget, the valuable ImageNet features. We avoid this with a two-phase schedule. In the first phase (epochs 1 to 5) the backbone is frozen and only the feature layer and the two heads are trained, at a learning rate of 1e-3, so that the heads learn to read the fixed ImageNet features without disturbing them. In the second phase (from epoch 6) all layers are unfrozen at a tenfold lower learning rate of 1e-4, allowing the backbone to specialise its mid- and high-level features to chest-X-ray textures gently, without destroying what pretraining provided. This schedule is the main reason the model converges quickly: its best validation checkpoint occurs at epoch 18, far earlier than the from-scratch model's epoch 60, because it begins from a strong initialisation rather than random noise.

5. Experiments and Results

All numbers reported below are measured on the held-out validation split at each model's best checkpoint, selected by macro AUC-ROC. The multi-label task is evaluated with per-class and macro AUC-ROC, and the binary task with AUC-ROC and F1.

5.1 Overall comparison

Table 4 reports the headline metrics for both models. The transfer-learned DenseNet is higher on every metric, reaching a macro AUC-ROC of 0.846 against 0.801 for the from-scratch network, a gain of 4.5 AUC-ROC points. It also converges far faster, reaching its best checkpoint at epoch 18 against epoch 60, despite carrying fewer trainable parameters. The DenseNet result edges past the CheXNet reference of 0.841, which we regard as confirmation that our pipeline is sound rather than as a meaningful improvement over that work.

5.2 Per-class performance

Per-class AUC-ROC for the DenseNet at its best epoch (Table 5) reveals a clear and clinically sensible pattern. The model is strongest on pathologies with a distinct global shape or high contrast, and weakest on diffuse, low-contrast findings. The top of the ranking is dominated by Edema (diffuse but high-contrast), Hernia and Cardiomegaly (gross changes in shape), and Emphysema (a clear lung-field texture). The bottom is occupied by Infiltration and Pneumonia, which are diffuse, subtle, and notoriously noisy in this dataset's report-mined labels. The low performance on Pneumonia in particular is probably due to the scarcity of positive examples (under 1% of films), and a lower ceiling on these classes is expected rather than a modelling failure, since human readers struggle with the same findings.

The two models differ in their ceiling: the DenseNet lifts the whole curve, most visibly on the mid-difficulty findings (Mass, Pneumothorax, Atelectasis), where richer pretrained features help to separate subtle structure from background.

5.4 Inference-time enhancements

The evaluation and demo paths add two techniques that incur no training cost and are available in the deployed system. Test-time augmentation averages the sigmoid probabilities over four views (the original, a horizontal flip, and rotations of plus and minus seven degrees), which reduces prediction variance. A two-model ensemble then averages the test-time-augmented probabilities of both models; because a from-scratch CNN and a pretrained DenseNet have different inductive biases and fail on different images, averaging their outputs is a standard and reliable way to recover a small amount of macro AUC over either model alone.

6. Discussion

Holding the data, task formulation, heads, loss, and evaluation fixed, the backbone and its initialisation are the only substantive variables, which lets us attribute the differences in Table 7 to architecture and pretraining. Transfer learning outperforms from-scratch training by about 4.5 macro AUC-ROC points while converging more than three times faster and training fewer parameters. This is the expected transfer-learning outcome, and reproducing it cleanly on a 112,000-image medical dataset under identical heads, loss, and evaluation is exactly the controlled comparison the project set out to make. The from-scratch model is not a failure: at 0.801 macro AUC-ROC it has clearly learned genuine pathology signal, and it serves its intended purpose as the baseline that quantifies what ImageNet pretraining is worth on this data.

6.1 Limitations

  • Label noise. ChestX-ray14 labels are mined from free-text radiology reports rather than verified lesion annotations, are assigned at the patient and report level, and are known to be noisy. This places a hard ceiling on the achievable AUC, especially for diffuse findings such as Infiltration and Pneumonia, our two weakest classes.
  • Validation, not test, figures. The numbers reported here are on the validation split used for checkpoint selection. The 22,000-image test split is held out; final unbiased test numbers should be computed before any external claim, and will typically sit slightly below the validation figures.
  • Unequal preprocessing between the reported runs. As stated in Section 5.3, the from-scratch checkpoint was trained without CLAHE and label smoothing, while the DenseNet checkpoint used both. The architectural conclusion is robust, but the precise margin is not a perfectly controlled ablation.
  • No external validation. Both models are trained and evaluated on a single institution's distribution. Performance is expected to degrade on images from different equipment, populations, or preprocessing, a well-documented generalisation gap for chest-X-ray models.
  • Not for clinical use. This is an educational system, and its predictions must not substitute for professional medical judgement.

6.2 Future work

  • Re-train the from-scratch CNN with CLAHE and label smoothing enabled, for a fully controlled scratch-versus-transfer ablation.
  • Report final held-out test metrics together with per-class confidence intervals.
  • Add Grad-CAM saliency overlays, implemented in the visualization module, to a sample of films for qualitative, clinically checkable interpretability.
  • Quantify the ensemble's macro-AUC gain numerically and tune per-class decision thresholds for the binary screening use case.

6.3 Reproducibility and engineering

The project is built to be reproduced rather than to produce a number once. Runs are deterministic, with a single seed across Python, NumPy, PyTorch, and CUDA and deterministic cuDNN. The data is pinned, with a dataset revision hash and a frozen 70/10/20 split embedded in source. All hyperparameters live in versioned YAML with inheritance, so nothing is hardcoded. The dispatcher bundles the working tree into the GPU kernel, so the cloud always runs the committed code. Every push triggers ruff linting, 51 pytest unit tests across nine modules, and mypy type checking. All training runs on free Kaggle GPUs, and the resulting artefacts (the checkpoint, the training history, and the model card) upload automatically to the model hub.

7. Conclusion

We built and compared two deep learning models for chest X-ray pathology detection on the 112,120-image NIH ChestX-ray14 database, each solving a fourteen-class multi-label task and a binary screening task through one shared backbone with two heads. A custom SE-ResNet trained entirely from scratch reached a macro AUC-ROC of 0.801, while a DenseNet-121 adapted by transfer learning reached 0.846, marginally surpassing the CheXNet reference and converging more than three times faster. The controlled comparison demonstrates the value of transfer learning for large-scale medical imaging, and the from-scratch baseline grounds that value in a concrete number. The per-class results agree with clinical intuition and with the known label-noise limits of the dataset, and the whole system is reproducible, tested, and deployed as a live demo. Although these results are encouraging, fully automated reading of chest radiographs from weakly supervised labels remains a difficult task, and the directions in Section 6.2 are the natural next steps.
